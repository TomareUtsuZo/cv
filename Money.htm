Money – An Introduction

 

For the last couple of weeks, I have been thinking about money. Not really something that is new for me, I often think about money and how it fits into the broader spectrum. What triggered this round was an interest in metal values and old 'junk' coins. But I'll get to that later. I figure it might be better to start at the beginning. What is money? Stop. Think for a second, what is money? Is it those green pieces of paper you pull from a slot at an ATM? Is it a number you look at online when checking the balance of your savings account. Maybe, it's gold or silver. Not quite. Money, in all forms, is a symbol for value.  Even 'real money' (aka gold and silver) is only a symbol.  Money symbolizes goods that have been produced that people are willing to trade for.  The value of money is how abundant the goods are in comparison to the demand for said goods. An example would be the hamburger.  To most people living in an urban environment the hamburger cost 89 cents from McDees and 12 bux from the Cheese Cake Factory. If Joe Blow Burgers start advertising 50 cent burgers across the street from McDees, the value of the Big Mac goes down (fewer people demanding the Big Mac), if the Government increases the minimum wage, either the prices of burgers goes up (which will drive demand down as people eat at home more often) and/or the supply of burgers will go down when restaurants go out of business.

So far, basic economics, right?  Next, what does the dollar mean to you. That really depends on how much money you earn. If you make six dollars an hour then a burger at The Cheese Cake Factory represents two hours of your life.  If you make twenty dollars an hour, then the same burger means about half an hour of your life.  If you are single and live with roommates, housing and utilities are a smaller portion of your income, therefore the less valuable each dollar is to you.  If you are married with children and only have ten percent of you income to 'spend' the more valuable each dollar is to you. Your wage represents how much society values the 'goods' you produce and how integral to the production said 'goods' you are. While health care is highly valued and part of a huge industry in the US registered nurses don't receive as much money as veterinarians do.  This is because the number of vets are so few so as to be able to demand higher pay for the work they do. This all ties into next weeks discussion on inflation. But for this week, think about what I said about what money means to you. I find it extreamly revealling when I think about possible purchases in the light of how many hours I must work to pay for it. So sit riveted to your seats, my fans.  :p

Money, Inflation and Deflation, pt 1
Category: News and Politics

For at least the first part of this discussion I am only going to talk about inflation and deflation in pure economic terms.  Later I will get into inflation and deflation in terms of money supply.

Inflation is where the value that must be traded to receive something increases.  For instance let's imagine that you lived on an island ten miles from the coast.  There is no bridge between the mainland and the island and the island is mostly self sufficient only trading for luxuries.  The farms on the island produce enough food so that a middle income family spends twenty percent of their income on food.  It has been this way for the last decade.  This basically means that the first hour and thirty-six minutes of the day are spent earning enough money to pay for food. But then suddenly for some reason there is a baby boom for a few years and the population of the island increases by twenty percent.  But the farms are only able to produce ten percent more food and some enterprising citizen starts a daily barge that buys food stuffs from the main land and brings them to the island.  Do to the costs involved and the increased demand for food, the number of hours per day that a middle income family must work to pay for the food raises to two hours per day.  This is inflation and is what happens when the amount of goods decreases or the demand for goods increase without a proportionate increase in production.

 
Deflation is where the value that must be traded to receive something decreases.  Imagine the same island as before after its baby boom, but ten years down the road.  Some bright person comes up with a way increase the yield of crops on the island by thirty percent while only increasing the price of food production by five percent.  At the end of the growing season, the markets are full of food and buyers demand lower prices.  The lower prices cause the person who owned the barge bringing food to go out of business and the prices of food drops twenty-five percent meaning that the aver rage middle income family now must only work one and a half hours to feed their family per day.  This is deflation and this happens when the demand for goods decreases, or the availability of goods increase.

 
Deflation and inflation can exist in the same economy at the same time.  For instance, in the first scenario it can be expected that the price of movie tickets when down when the price of food went up.  This would happen because people would have less money to spend on going to the movies and the movie theater would want to keep from going out of business, so the lower prices.  Of course, the movie theater would also probably have to start offering movies that aren't first run and maybe change the hours of operation as well.  In the second scenario it would be able increase the price of admission because of the increased availability of disposable income and may be able to use the extra revenue to show first run movies and show at more convenient hours.

 
So, I hope this was interesting.  But I think I will put off talking about inflation as it applies to the Gross Domestic Product (GDP) till later.  I think a page is enough.

September 3, 2007 - Monday

Some more on the price of goods
Current mood:  cheerful
Category: News and Politics

Inflation isn't always real.  Or what we sometimes call inflation isn't really inflation.  In the US we have for a long time been in a state of deflation (mostly and more often then not) even though the prices of goods have risen.  I am not ready to start discussing why this is happening in the US (it's complicated.) But I will discuss some of the concepts involved.

At its most basic, an increase in prices of goods that comes with an increase in income (aka a decrease in the amount of work/time needed to earn said goods) is not actually inflation.    If a meal at The Cheesecake Factory cost 10% more but the amount of time you must work to earn the money to pay for that meal decreases by 20% then economically, you have deflation, not inflation.  Similarly, if    you must work two hours to pay for a meal that you used to be able to pay for with only one hour of work, then it doesn't matter if the price of the meal was reduced 50% you are still experiencing 100% inflation.

What sort of conditions might lead to a deflation dispite an increase in prices?  A trade surplus could do it.  Trade surpluses exist when a trading bloc (the US could be considered a trading bloc.  Arizona could be considered a trading bloc.) sells more goods to other trade bloc's then it buys from other trade bloc's.   Should that happen there would be a general increase in wealth within that bloc.  That there is now more money available for purchases will cause an increase in prices as more people will seek to buy more goods.  The increase in prices will generally result in an increase in availability of goods as producers within and without the bloc shift investments to produce more of the goods.  That will lead to a greater availability of goods to the people at prices slightly higher before, but generally lower then the increase in wages.  Often this natural market mechanism results in the trade surplus turning into a trade balance because outside producers of goods will have the advantage of lower labor costs.

An example of this would be our aforementioned island of economic principles.  (I think I'll name it Eco for short.)  Lets say that historically Eco has been known for the quality of the wing-dings it makes. Do to this quality there is generally more demand then supply and it is easier for the wing-ding producers to set the prices.  The baby boomers are now entering the job market and the increase in labor has increased production of wing-dings which continue to be a hot commodity.  Because of the increased wealth of the island the people of Eco want more stuff and because they have the money, they stand willing to pay more for it.  For instance, target shooting might become all the rage of boomers with too much time on their hands.  This would push the price of ammo up leading to the ammo factory modernizing its facilities to produce more ammo and mail order ammo doing a brisk business covering the rest.  Technically, prices went up, but the island is no less rich for it.  

Another way prices could increase without true inflation happening is if the government was to print more money even though hadn't been an equal increase in goods produced by Eco.  Let's say they printed 10% more money even though the availablity of goods had only increased 5%.  It could be expected that the price of goods will have increased by 5% but the real price those goods (as determined by amount of time spent working to pay for them) of had actually dropped by 5%.
