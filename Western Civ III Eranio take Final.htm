The Great Depression:

How Capitalism Was Defeated

 

By: Carl Eranio

 

06Jun2005

 

 

 

 

The Great Depression.  The name invokes many images of darkness and hopelessness.  Possibly you think of your grandparent’s unreasonable fear of placing their money in the bank.  Maybe you could hear reverence in their voices when speaking of such hero’s as John Maynard Keynes and Franklin D. Roosevelt.  Do pictures come to mind of bankrupted businessmen throwing themselves out of their office windows?  Perhaps you have seen the old newsreels of long lines at the soup kitchen, out-of-work men listlessly sitting around without the energy to do anything. 1

 

Invariably, the question of how to make sure this never happens again rises, and so one must ask what was the cause of this catastrophe.  Many point the finger at unbridled capitalism as the cause and tout socialism as the cure.  This, however, is not true.  Instead, it was a combination of nationalism and socialism interfering with natural economic rhythms that caused the Great Depression to be Great.  The Great Depression was the result of human errors in the area of economic policy.  A great many mistakes were made by well-meaning people who were grossly ignorant about economics.

 

We should start our search for the truth by exploring the ways the First World War played a role in laying the groundwork for future economic troubles.  Damage and changes the war wrought on the world economy, plus the apparent success of increased government involvement in matters of economics during the war, laid the groundwork for future economic troubles.  First, we will talk about the direct and indirect damages done by the war.  3

Easily the most recognizable damage done to the world economy was the amount of death and destruction created by the Great War.  France, one of the hardest hit countries, had 8% of its territory, accounting for over 30% of its prewar industrial production, occupied by the German army, who deliberately destroyed much of those industries as they retreated back to Germany.4 France and Germany suffered casualties of approximately 10% of their male work force.5  These figures help us to see that Europe was facing a uphill battle to regain economic stability. 

 

The war also stressed out the economy; adding to the troubles created by the damaging first World War.  With everyone expecting the war to be “won by Christmas” 1914, most governments chose not to raise taxes to pay for the costs of waging war and

instead took loans to pay for the war.6  Even after it became evident that the war was 3. going to continue sometime governments continued to follow this policy as a propaganda measure.  This policy led to inflation throughout Europe.  In Britain, who got off lightly compared to the rest of Europe, prices roughly doubled.7 Germany increased its public debt thirty fold between 1914 and 1918, from 5.4 billion marks to 156.1 billion marks,8 flooding the economy with money.  France suffered a wholesale price increase of 350% during the war.9  Lost, too, was money of internationally but never recovered.  An infamous example is the nine billion francs lent by the French government to Tsarist Russia.  After the war the Bolshevik Government would not honor debts of the Tsarist government.10  Finally, was the cost in production.  The following is an index of German industrial production from 1913 through 1923. 11

Germany’s “economy bottomed out in 1919 because, even though hostilities were over, the blockade remained in effect until July 1919.  Output was about 62% lower in 1919 then in 1913.” 12

 

The sizable amount of debt created innumerable problems for currency stability; making it critical that trade resume so the governments could pay for their debts.  There are two important problems with sizable government debt by the rules of classic economic philosophy followed at the time.  One is that it created inflationary conditions without a growth in economic output.  This was back before corporate structures had developed strategies to use inflation to limit the damages that organized labor did to its ability to respond to market conditions.  Second was that a government that could not balance its books could not operate on the gold standard for long.  This was important as it was assumed by all that a return to the gold standard after the war was a when not an if.  Therefore any nation that could not pay its debt would be seen by capital as risky and be

 

Classical economics was based upon two key factors.  The first was the gold standard and the second was a government that did not set economic policies.  The first thing to understand about the gold standard is that in order for it to work the rules of the game must be followed.  And if the rules of the game were followed then government has almost not control over the domestic supply of money.  Now as briefly touched on earlier, when an economy increases the amount of money, inflation happens. When the amount of money in an economy falls, the value of what money still remains (people who saved their money) increases as the prices of goods and services decreases as the suppliers of those goods and services become more competitive or go out of business.  This is equally true without a gold standard currency; however, if the currency is pegged to a standard the government has guaranteed that it will supply a set amount of a commodity (gold) for every unit of money.  If a government was to increase the supply of money without having enough of that commodity available then no one would trade with that currency anymore out of fear of the government not being able to supply the demanded commodity.  Obviously not government would want this so it would not print money it could not pay for.  13

 

Restarting trade faced an uphill battle to overcome the damaged done to their industries and a changed continental and world trade situation.  Before the war began  

Europe was the center of world trade, accounting for over 50 percent of the trade worldwide.  While Europe was at war with itself countries whose involvement it was limited, notably the United States and Japan, were able to increase their share of the world market.  Japan refined its silk production methods significantly enough to export to both the Americas and Europe.  Even more troubling was Japans ability to competitively export its textiles to Europe; taking up markets that Europe wasn’t used to competing for.  The United States increased its share of exports to Latin America by more than three quarters.  The critical wartime industries of ship building, iron and steel refining, and coal mining were not able to compete in a market saturated from over production.  A problem made worse by the increased power of labor. 14

 

This increased power of labor is part of the problems created by increased government handling of economic decisions, a feature of the way the war changed the view of what role governments had in the economic process.  After World War One ended the increase role of the masses necessitated socialist politics to sway the voters.  The reason for this is, while excitedly drafting there constitutions, the newly come into being republics made two notable mistakes.  The first was that they assumed there was no higher good then the will of the people, there by giving them the power of tyrants.  The second problem was to create constitutions that would allow parliaments to become fragmented and unable to act. 

 

 

 

 

These two problems had dire political, economic, and civil consequences.  Civilly, giving the newly empowered masses, when the could work together, dictatorial powers was a mistake that necessitated socialist politics to sway voters.  The Spanish constitution, an extreme example, stated in writing that property may be stolen from its owner “for social uses.”15  Later the wealthy and upper class revolted against this and threw the country into a bloody civil war rather then have their property stolen.  Unfortunately, these powers did not require them selves to be written out as a precondition of use.  In order to buy off the masses, who might have been tempted to institute Soviet Bolshevisms, the parceling out of land seized from the political minority to create a new, larger, class of land-owning peasant happened with frightening regularity.  This was effective as it was wrong.  What made it so effective was that it created a larger base of voters who now had something to lose.  Where before they had nothing to lose by the abolition of personal property, now it made sense to back more conservative political platform.  Czechoslovakia and the Balkans used this strategy.16  This was the less extreme version of what Nazi Germany would eventually do to the Jews.  This, too, had economic consequences when the markets became glutted with excess food the greater number of smaller suppliers was un-competitive. 

 

Politically, problems arouse when parliamentary bodies could not come to a consensus of the correct action to take.  The writing of constitutions to limit the power of

the executive branch made even more damaging the fragmentation of the parliamentary body by proportional representation.  This created government bodies unable to act to govern the nation when a consensus.  Populous’ and parliaments, both, frustrated at the inability of government to solve anything through assemblies of the public official began transferring power to their executive branch.  “Legislative paralysis,” according to Mortiz Bonn, “has created a clamor for a dictator …”17 Eventually, Germany and Italy succumbed to the siren song of a stable dictatorship vice a paralyzed parliamentary body. 18

 

A very good example of political and economic problems that arouse from the parliamentary dependence on the masses good will is the French economic crisis.  A key aspect of inflation in Germany and France was the war reparations of the Treaty of Versailles.  The German Chancellors (Wirth 1921-1922 and Cuno 1922-23) viewed hyperinflation as a bargaining chip to for reducing or abolishing its war reparations and postponed balancing the budget.  For the French the reparations represented an opportunity to cheat on the budget.  Instead of balancing the budget through fiscal conservatism and/or increased taxes, the French parliament chose to rely on the war reparations to balance the budget.  With the Germans defaulting on its payments and the French economy suffering from the continued inflation created by a deficit budget France elected in 1923 to invade the Ruhr.  For the French this proved disastrous.  The costs of

occupying the Ruhr were increased by the passive resistance of the German people to the point where it cost more then it gained worsening the budget, caused the to abandon the Ruhr before the year was out.19  With this plan backfiring the French plan to use the reparations to balance the budget failed creating a huge debt to pay for the upgrades and rebuilding of its industries instead of taking a slower, longer route of increased taxes and balanced budgets.  All this paying of government debt by printing more money led to rising inflation and major depreciation of the franc.  Finally forced, in 1924, to face reality, they raised the taxes and achieved financial stability by cutting government spending.  The voters voted out the conservatives and voted in socialist politicians in order to get the tax system to take the money from the wealthy and give it the lower class. This too ended up being disastrous for the French when a major political battle erupted over who to tax and how that money should be spent, paralyzing the government for two years.  The country went through ten different ministers of finance in almost as many governments. Worse, that major taxes aimed at the wealthy could come at anytime moved them to send their wealth out of country to protect their interest.  Not being able to find anybody to buy their debt forced the government to print even more money causing inflation of 350%. 20

 

The French parliament finally managed, after the some of the left broke ranks and joined the conservatives in July 1926, to achieve financial stabilization.  The lowering of taxes on income brought back capital into the country, appreciating the franc from

240 francs/pound to 190 francs/pound within a week’s time.  Raising indirect taxes and cutting the deficit further appreciated the franc to 124/pound; where it was pegged on the gold standard.21  The French economic crisis and its resolution is a good example of how governments where forced to, by the induction of the “non-revolutionary working class”, to find “political legitimacy in continuing level of economic activity” to the collective advantage of its electorate22  This increased activity increased the size of the government infrastructure.  It’s important to note this as it means that government now cost more money and served as a link to strengthen the importance of nationalism in political responses to economic problems.23

 

 

The cost of the increased government involvement in economic matters can be seen in a couple situations.  In a free-market condition an industrialist will seek to barrow money from capitalists.  This person or organization will then ask the industrialist how he plans to use this money to make more money.  If the capitalist sees sense in the plan he will invest in the industrialist’s plan and charge an interest rate that represents a combination of the risk involved with the lending of his capital and the loss of utility that money could have had if he had kept hold of it for some other use.  Because the money was given to the government that is run by people who don’t have any stake in the success or failure of the venture the money was not properly invested.  The Dawes Plan

funneled 800 million marks into the German government, which inefficiently used the money.  The most stagnant part of the industries, iron, steel, textiles, agriculture, and even municipalities (like the City of Cologne) used their political muscle to secure more money than was wise in a free market sense.24  The French government, in its reconstruction efforts was not as effective as it could have been when it only brought their factories back up to their pre-war state instead of taking this opportunity to fully modernize, leaving them uncompetitive on the international scene.25  Some governments tried to create unemployment insurance, but this was a persistent drain on the state budget.  And as we saw, this was painful for a gold standard based economy, especially when the question of who would pay for the benefits was raised.  More evidence of the cost of government attempts at providing investment capital to industry can be seen in the failure of Eastern Europe to become competitive.  Its subsidies, tax breaks, high tariffs, and the like propped up local industries instead of forcing them to modernize and become competitive.26  Lastly was the way government would use foreign investment capital to pay for civil services.  In Romania one-third of the investment money was spent on services and projects to generate prestige.  Half the money invested in Hungary, Bulgaria, Yugoslavia, and Poland was used to import goods and services.27  Germany spent large amounts of foreign investment money on “modernizing civic life” in the form of: public transportation, swimming pools, sports stadiums, and exhibition halls.  Even Britain was spending money on public works instead of leaving that money in the market. 28

 

This, possibly, seems perfectly natural in today’s world of debt, unbalanced budgets, and inflation.  However, it is directly related to the cause of the Great Depression in that the governments should have used the money to pay their public debts.  Doing so would have placed the investment capital back into the hands of the people who would have cared the most about the economic viability of an investment.  Money, instead of being wasted on inefficient industries or unprofitable public works, would have found its way into industries that created money, trade, and jobs.  People would have been forced to adapt or go under and the resulting markets would have eventually worked themselves out.

 

US Federal Reserve board was another major piece of the puzzle regarding what it was supposed to be, what it was, and what it was not.  The Fed was established in 1914 as a way to save banks from going under do to bank runs.  Its job was to provide banks that were facing a temporary liquidity problem (Not having enough cash to fill requests for withdrawals due to having their money tied up in loans.) with cash to see them through the bank run until liquidity was restored.  It was not intended to act as a central bank.

Unfortunately, as with all government attempts to solve a problem, it did not work as planned.  It did not save banks at all.  Instead, it was used by banks a way to get more money to loan out.29   The obvious result of this is an increased monetary base in the economy without the normal economic controls to make sure that money was only used in economically responsible ways.  When money is readily available through loans, investments that normally look risky become more attractive as the cost of money is lower then the potential gain.  When the only money that is available is the money you have or a very expensive loan people will take fewer risks when investing.

 

Aside from that, it meant that money flowed into the hands of only the rich disrupting the normal distribution patterns further unbalancing the economy.  The rich spend their money differently then the poor.  The poor receive a much greater level of satisfaction from every dollar he receives as compared to a rich person.  A poor person who spends half his paycheck will have a greater emotional attachment to what he received because that half a paycheck will buy fewer things.  If a rich person was to spend half his income he would only get some more satisfaction from that purchase as compared to the poor person who bought something that was much cheaper.  So, a rich person will first look at his income and ask himself how to use most of his income to make more money.  The poor person will ask himself what he can now afford with what he has.  Money that only the rich will get will not affect the economy like it will affect it if the poor also gain some of it.

 

Possibly more damaging, was the Feds stepping out of its assigned role and stepping into what would become know as macroeconomics.   It began this experiment while the Great War was being fought, when it saw the US gold stock increase by about 90%, it sold a large number of government bonds to buy this gold inflow to remove this money from circulation rather then allowing inflation to happen.30  Before heading back to Europe, and how those policies affected their economies, I want to show how the Fed placed the final nails in the coffin.  When the depression arrived in the states, and started toppling banks, instead of saving those banks, as it was created to do, it allowed them to go under by not loaning them the money to stay solvent and even took steps to reduce the money base during an economic contraction.   It was these actions that turned the American depression into the Great Depression.31

 

The return to the gold standard has drawn a lot of criticism as being largely responsible for causing the Great Depression.  People will point to the British Empires return at pre-war parity and the sluggish economy that followed.  Others point to the French undervalued franc and its exacerbation of the British problem.  Let’s look at how the pound/dollar relationship should have worked itself out in response to the British

returning to the gold standard overvalued.  By making the pound 10% too expensive in relation to the dollar they made their own goods too expensive by 10% and made United States goods 10% cheaper.  This would create a trade deficit where in which United States goods would flow into the United Kingdom; while British gold would flow into the United States economy.  The rising amount of money in the United States would cause inflation while the falling stock of gold would lead to deflation in the United Kingdom.  After a period of economic adjustment, a trade parity would be achieved. IF the rules of the game were followed.  And the United States had not regulated the amount of money in the economy. 33

 

When Britain returned to the gold standard it returned at the pre-war parity of 4.86 pounds to the dollar it, did so as a policy of nationalism.  The thought process behind this decision was that, to use the pre-war rate would help reestablish the countries financial leadership in the world.  As we learned earlier, this forced the empire into a deflationary economic situation.  This was even more damaging in light of the over production that the world was facing already and hit hard what was previously its most valuable industries.  Eventually, though, the gold standard would have evened everything out and Britain would have become competitive again.  IF the rules of the game had been followed.  And so the British felt that a tightening of the belt would eventually be worth it in their return the top of the financial world.  Instead, the United States and France (who, in a politically motivated move, returned at a parity that was 25% undervalued.34) were busy not allowing the gold flowing into their economy to increase the amount of money and so the United Kingdom wasn’t able to ever recover economically while they were on the gold standard.  It was the fear of the political fall out that would result from inflation that caused the elected officials of French government to sterilize the gold flow.35/36.

 

It was countries returning to the gold standard in ways that showed a lack of respect for market factors that created massive problems for European ability to recover economically.  Not that France and the United States were the only nations to return to a gold parity that was undervalued.  Belgium chose a rate that was undervalued to the pound.  Norway, Denmark, and Italy chose to return at a overvalued rate that slowed their economy down in a time when they needed to grow to keep up with their debts. Couple this with a debt structure tied all nations together, and the world was just waiting for the Great Depression to come along and tear down the house of cards that was being held up on loans and laws. 37

 

            The nations in place increasingly turned to nationalism to deal with economic troubles.  Their inability to see past their differences and cooperate economically, to take a true laissez faire attitude about the economy, led to the flourishing of economic nationalism.  A laissez faire attitude regarding trade, instead of a protectionist policy would have forced uncompetitive industries to either become competitive enough to survive the world market or go under allowing capital to be channeled into more profitable venues. 38 When America failed to join the League of Nations it created a painful back drop in European/United States relations.  Europe felt it had been left in the lurch by the US and would point to its “irresponsibility” as good reason to distrust the US.  Also, the lack of the US in the League allowed the French to get away with maintaining the Treaty of Versailles as a way to keep the Germans down.39  Italian Prime Minister, Francisco Nitti, pointed out “the keen contest of nationalism, land-grabbing and cornering of raw materials renders friendly relations between the thirty states of Europe extremely difficult.”  Nationalism was even further embedded in mindsets of the nations through the way that the treaty bound some nations to not cooperate.40 

 

            Tariffs and protectionist policies made trade and economic recovery difficult to achieve.  The US, through the 20’s maintained an average tariff of one-third on its products, making it difficult for Europe to compete with them, in order to placate powerful political groups.  Europe, too, succumbed to pressure to ‘protect’ national markets from international competition.  Through the use of direct (tariffs and import quotas) and indirect protection (subsidies and sanitary restrictions as examples) they

slowly placed ever increasingly large walls against trade that was needed to create revenues and competitive efficiency in a damaged post war economic environment.41 The Locarno Treaty of 1925 returned to Germany the ability to place protectionist policies for its markets and it immediately returned to its pre-1914 levels of protection. Norway, the same year, imposed measures to protect its lumberjack industries.  Italy pointed to the way of the future with its ‘Battle for Wheat’ that highlighted the strength of agricultures problems of remain solvent in the face of international competition and the rise of Nationalism.  In 1927 alone, Western Europe created tariffs to protect agriculture that ranged from 15 to 50 percent in Germany, France, Italy, Belgium, Switzerland, Austria, Sweden, and Finland. 

 

            Eastern and central Europe was just as protectionist in their policies.  Austria placed protections in 1924, followed by Hungary and Yugoslavia.  In comparison Austria was at least nodding to free trade with their much lower protections while the other two placed barriers to trade that was in large excess to the pre-war levels.  Bulgaria and Romania wrote into laws tariffs that protected every field of industry their countries had active, ranging from 50 to 300 percent.  As the level of supply burgeoned and suppliers became desperate to sell at any price, governments began using creative methods of protecting domestic markets.  First introduced in Norway was the ‘Milling Ratio’ that made it so that no matter how much sellers lowered their prices they could not sell their products.42

 

            Actions and policies in America again caused massive damage to the cause of international trade.  The United States accounted for more then 12% of the world’s total imports and was the largest foreign lender of capital to the world.  In 1927 the Fed finally released a significant portion of the money it had been hoarding into the market.  This gesture of good will to the United Kingdom, intended to help stop the gold out flow from British markets, is attributed with causing the stock market boom that proceeded the United States joining the slumping world economy.  The stock market boom caused United States’ investors to stop investing in foreign markets and start speculating on stocks.43  Without the United States’ investments in their countries they were now faced with the loss of capital to continue paying for their economic recovery.  These recessions caused world exports to fall even further hurting exporting countries.44  Hardest hit was Germany who was almost all debt.  Things were made worse when, as the American economy was starting to slide, it introduced increasing protectionist policies that reduced its imports further damaging European countries ability to make money by trade.  Possibly the most famous of these was, the Smoot-Hawley, a tariff introduced in 1929 and passed as law in 1930.

            The damage that protectionist economic policies cause is two fold.  First is the well-known way that it reduces or makes more difficult for nations to trade with a protected economy.  But there is a secondary way that it damages an economy in the international and domestic sense.  If it does what its job then prices will rise on the protected products in country.  This is good for the suppliers but then the rising prices of goods-causes the labor pool to start demanding higher wages to keep up with the increasing cost of living.  The increased cost of wages cuts the profits of both the protected industries and non-protected industries and they again find themselves failing to make money.  The increased cost of production makes the country less competitive on the international market as well further hurting the countries economy.  Also when an industry, such as agriculture, is protected and the price of goods rise, more suppliers increase their production leading to depressed prices again.  Lastly is the problem of retaliatory protectionism on the part of a countries potential trading partners.  If country A can’t export to country B then A will pass laws to protect their markets until a whirlwind of nationalism destroys both countries (or in the case of Europe leads to the rise of the Third Reich and war.) They fed off each other in an ever increasingly vicious cycle.45  Protectionism and socialism and are related to each other in that protectionism comes into being when a political group is threatened by an outside source.  This political group gets the government to help keep it competitive.  An illustration of this is the heavy industries of the United Kingdom.  After the war they could no longer compete against outside sources.  The capitalist’s responded by trying to cut costs.  Cutting costs involves firing unneeded employees and reducing wages.  The increased political power of the labor party would create a call to protect jobs.  Laws would be enacted to protect the heavy industries.  And heavy industries would not become competitive.  All this protectionism shows the relationship between Nationalism and Socialism. 

 

            In conclusion, the unbridled capitalism that caused the Great Depression did not exist in interwar Europe.  To many political groups had their hands in the pot.  Politicians sacrificed large land-owning minorities, or turned to Fascism, to appease potential Bolshevik revolutionaries.  Parliaments couldn’t take action to repair a broken budget without getting voted out until the whole nation was being destroyed by Inflation.  Protectionist economics destroyed any possibility of trade in an environment in desperate need of trade to pay for debts created by the war.
